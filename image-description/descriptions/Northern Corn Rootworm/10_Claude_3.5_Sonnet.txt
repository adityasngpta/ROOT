These pests typically feed on the upper, sun-exposed foliage of plants, which can lead to reduced photosynthesis and overall plant health if left unchecked.

To manage Japanese Beetle infestations, a multi-pronged approach is recommended. First, manual removal of the beetles can be effective for small populations. Shaking plants early in the morning when the beetles are less active can cause them to fall into a bucket of soapy water. For larger infestations, consider using insecticidal treatments containing pyrethrins or neem oil, which are effective against Japanese Beetles while being relatively safe for beneficial insects. 

Long-term management strategies include planting less attractive species to Japanese Beetles, such as magnolias or lilacs, and encouraging natural predators like birds. Pheromone traps can be used to monitor beetle populations but should be placed away from valuable plants to avoid attracting more beetles to the area. Lastly, treating the soil with milky spore disease or beneficial nematodes can help control the larval stage of the beetles, reducing future adult populations. Regular monitoring and early intervention are key to preventing severe damage from these persistent pests.
