Northern Corn Rootworm beetles can cause significant damage to corn crops, primarily in their larval stage when they feed on corn roots. While adult beetles like the one pictured feed on corn silks, pollen, and leaves, their damage is usually less severe than that caused by the larvae. However, their presence indicates a potential for future crop damage if left unchecked.

To manage Northern Corn Rootworm infestations, a multi-faceted approach is recommended. Crop rotation is one of the most effective strategies, as it disrupts the pest's life cycle. Rotating corn with non-host crops like soybeans can significantly reduce rootworm populations. For fields where rotation is not feasible, consider using corn hybrids with built-in rootworm resistance. Soil-applied insecticides at planting can also be effective in controlling larval populations. For adult beetles, foliar insecticides may be necessary if populations exceed economic thresholds, typically when silk clipping becomes severe. It's crucial to scout fields regularly, especially during silking, to monitor beetle populations and assess the need for treatment. Additionally, implementing conservation practices that promote natural predators can help keep rootworm populations in check. Always consult with local agricultural extension services for specific recommendations tailored to your region and the current pest pressure.
