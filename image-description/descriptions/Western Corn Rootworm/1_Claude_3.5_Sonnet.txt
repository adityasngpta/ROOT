Western Corn Rootworms can cause significant damage to corn crops, both in their larval and adult stages. The larvae feed on corn roots, reducing the plant's ability to take up water and nutrients, while adults, like the one pictured, feed on corn silks and leaves, potentially interfering with pollination and photosynthesis.

To manage this infestation, a multi-faceted approach is recommended. Crop rotation is one of the most effective strategies, as it disrupts the rootworm's life cycle. Rotating corn with non-host crops like soybeans can significantly reduce rootworm populations. If rotation is not possible, consider using corn varieties that express Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) proteins toxic to rootworms. Soil-applied insecticides at planting can also help control larval populations. For adult beetles, foliar insecticides may be necessary if populations exceed economic thresholds, typically around 0.75 to 1 beetle per plant. Additionally, monitoring for rootworm activity using yellow sticky traps can help inform treatment decisions. It's also crucial to practice good field hygiene, removing crop residues that can harbor overwintering eggs. Lastly, consider working with local agricultural extension services to develop a comprehensive integrated pest management strategy tailored to your specific situation and region.
