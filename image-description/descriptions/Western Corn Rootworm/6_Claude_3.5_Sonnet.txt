Western Corn Rootworms can cause significant damage to corn crops, particularly in their larval stage when they feed on corn roots. While the adult beetles, like the one pictured, primarily feed on corn silks and leaves, their presence indicates a potential for future root damage if left unchecked. The feeding activity of adults can interfere with pollination and reduce yield if populations are high.

To manage this pest, a multi-faceted approach is recommended. Crop rotation is one of the most effective strategies, as it disrupts the rootworm's life cycle. Rotating corn with non-host crops like soybeans can significantly reduce rootworm populations. If rotation is not possible, consider using corn hybrids with rootworm-resistant traits. For immediate control of adult populations, foliar insecticides may be applied, but this should be done only if economic thresholds are reached.

Monitoring is crucial for effective management. Farmers should regularly scout their fields, checking for adult beetles on corn leaves and silks. Yellow sticky traps can also be used to monitor adult populations. If larval damage is suspected, dig up corn plants and examine the roots for feeding injury. Implementing an Integrated Pest Management (IPM) program that includes cultural, biological, and chemical control methods will provide the best long-term solution for managing Western Corn Rootworm infestations while minimizing environmental impact and preserving beneficial insects.
