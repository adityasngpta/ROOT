While the leaf itself appears mostly intact, Japanese Beetles are known to cause significant damage to foliage through their feeding habits. They tend to skeletonize leaves, consuming the soft tissue between the veins, which can lead to a lace-like appearance of the damaged leaves. If the infestation is severe, this feeding damage can result in reduced photosynthetic capacity, stunted growth, and potential yield loss.

To manage and control Japanese Beetle infestations, several strategies can be employed:

1. Mechanical control: Physically remove and destroy the beetles by handpicking or using traps. Japanese Beetle traps can be effective in reducing local populations.

2. Biological control: Encourage natural predators such as birds, predatory insects, and beneficial nematodes that feed on Japanese Beetle grubs in the soil.

3. Chemical control: Apply insecticides labeled for Japanese Beetle control, following the manufacturer's instructions and safety precautions. Insecticides containing active ingredients such as carbaryl, pyrethroids, or neonicotinoids can be effective.

4. Cultural practices: Maintain healthy plants through proper irrigation, fertilization, and pruning, as vigorous plants are more resilient to pest damage. Remove and destroy heavily infested plant material to reduce the spread of the infestation.

5. Timing: Focus control efforts on the adult stage of the Japanese Beetle life cycle, typically during their peak activity in summer months.

Implementing an integrated pest management (IPM) approach that combines multiple control methods is often the most effective way to manage Japanese Beetle infestations while minimizing the reliance on chemical insecticides.
